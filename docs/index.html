Abstract

We train a Random Forest Classifier on five legally available, non-leaky search console and web engagement features from March 2026 to predict whether search impressions will decline by more than 20% in April 2026. Evaluating our model under an honest, client-grouped split (72,470 train / 28,971 test, 0 overlapping clients), we find that a simple rule-based baseline (Precision@50 = 92.0%) outperforms all trained ML models at the top of the ranked queue — best: Logistic Regression at 62.0%, Random Forest at 52.0% — while the baseline's overall ROC AUC (0.5226) remains the weakest of the four. This counterintuitive result demonstrates why grouped validation matters more than headline model complexity.

01 — Question

Can we predict search decay before it shows up in the dashboard?

Research question: can we predict upcoming organic search decay for a highly visible content item using only its local historical performance and engagement signals — and prioritize these items in a ranked queue that outperforms static heuristic rules?

In large-scale digital publishing, content operations teams face a screening prioritisation bottleneck. High-authority evergreen pages represent major traffic and revenue assets, but they naturally decay as search trends shift and competitor content matures. Updating pages is labor-intensive — editors reviewing pages randomly, or by simplistic rules, waste hours auditing healthy pages while missing visible assets on the brink of collapse.

This research supports the content refresh prioritization decision: ranking the entire portfolio continuously so editors can pull exactly the top K pages most in need of a refresh, matching their weekly operational bandwidth.

02 — Data

A two-month window, one filter, no private data

We use the FlyRank Warehouse Release (v20260703), hosted on Hugging Face, spanning distinct brand portfolios. To enforce prediction-time discipline:

  • Feature / decision window — March 1–31, 2026 (month = '2026-03'). Every input feature is aggregated purely over this month.
  • Outcome / label window — April 1–30, 2026 (month = '2026-04'). Decay is evaluated solely on April's search console impressions.

Population filters: pages must earn at least 100 organic impressions in March, and rows lacking a GSC position or a content creation date are dropped. This leaves 101,441 active content items.

Exclusions: all PII, brand domains, URLs, titles, and raw search queries are excluded. Identifiers are stable, scrambled pseudonyms (client_hash_id, content_hash_id). No product-derived composite decisions — existing priority scores or app-generated flags — are used as features, to avoid learning historical administrative rules instead of the world.

03 — Methodology

Five honest features, one frozen baseline, two validation designs

Features

mar_impressionsTotal organic Search Console impressions earned in March.
mar_clicksTotal organic Search Console clicks earned in March.
mar_avg_positionImpression-weighted average Search Console position in March.
mar_ga4_sessions_per_dayAverage GA4 organic sessions per day, on days with active tracking.
content_age_daysDays since content creation, as of March 31, 2026.

All five are knowable strictly before the label window — none are derived from the outcome.

Target

is_declining = 1 if April impressions fall below 80% of March impressions (or the page disappears entirely in April), else 0. Test-set base rate: 51.7%.

Baseline (frozen, Week 4)

A transparent, hand-written rule, kept unchanged through every later assignment: a page is flagged when it is stale (content_age_days ≥ 365) and visible (mar_impressions ≥ 500), scored by its raw impression count — score = stale × visible × mar_impressions.

Validation design

We compare a stratified random split against a client-grouped split — entire brands held out, so no client's pages appear in both train and test. The grouped design tests generalization to genuinely unseen brands, and prevents the model from faking skill by memorizing brand-specific configurations.

Leakage audit

We deliberately inject apr_impressions — the outcome-window metric — into the training features. Retraining under this leaked feature drives ROC AUC to a near-perfect 0.9993, confirming our validation harness is sensitive to leakage and that the honest five-feature set is properly insulated from it.

The baseline wins decisively at the top of the ranked list. It achieves Precision@20 = 1.00 and Precision@50 = 0.92 — nearly every one of its top 50 picks was genuinely declining. The best ML model at this cutoff, Logistic Regression, trails at 0.62 — a 30-point gap.

But ranking-wide, the baseline is the weakest model. Its ROC AUC (0.5226) is the lowest of the four, while Random Forest (0.6046) and Decision Tree (0.5936) rank better across the whole test set. The baseline only wins because it correctly isolates a small, unambiguous set of "big, old, high-impression" pages at the very top — it doesn't generalize as a full ranking.

Decision Tree is a cautionary case. Despite the second-best ROC AUC, its Precision@20/@50 (0.20/0.20) sits at roughly the base rate — a reminder that overall ranking skill and top-K precision can diverge sharply, and a single metric can mislead.

Practical takeaway: for this task, at these specific cutoffs, the simple frozen baseline is genuinely hard to beat at the very top of the queue. Any real value from the ML models here would need to be demonstrated deeper in the ranking, not in the headline top-20/top-50 numbers.

05 — Limitations

What this work cannot claim

  1. Observational, not causal. This work documents observed associations between historical performance and search decline. We cannot claim that executing a suggested action — expanding content depth, say — will cause a recovery. That requires a controlled, randomized test against a matched control group.
  2. Unbalanced history footprint. Client historical depth varies wildly, which can skew features for younger sites compared to established assets.
  3. External variables excluded. Core algorithm updates, competitor activity, seasonal shifts, and industry-wide volume shocks aren't captured in local Search Console metrics — predictions remain subject to sudden external shocks.
